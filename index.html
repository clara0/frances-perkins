About Frances Perkins Day

Who was Frances Perkins?

Frances Perkins strove to improve working conditions and increase wages, particularly for women. She also wanted to implement a minimum wage as well as a maximum workweek to cap the number of hours women and children could work in a week. After the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire in which many young women died, she began to fight for improved fire safety as well. Throughout her career, she fought to end child labor and improve insurance for the unemployed. She also helped develop a social security policy to provide retired people with an income.


Her Achievements

Frances Perkins was able to successfully achieve a variety of reforms that she tirelessly worked towards, including a minimum wage, maximum workweek, and better working conditions for women and children. One of her earlier achievements was the passage of the 54-hour bill after extensive lobbying, which would limit the workweek of women and children to fifty-four hours a week. After being appointed the secretary of labor by President Theodore Roosevelt, she was key in the creation of the Social Security Act and Fair Labor Standards Act. The Social Security Act created a program that provided regular payments for retired people above sixty-five years old. The Fair Labor Standards Act established a starting minimum wage of twenty-five cents an hour, and required that wage to be increased to forty cents an hour within the next seven years. It also dictated that no person should be required to work more than forty-four hours a week the first year of employment, forty-two hours a week the second year, and forty hours a week for all other years without overtime pay.

Her Impact on Work Today

While Frances Perkins is often overshadowed by other reformers from the Progressive Era, the accomplishments she made during her lifetime still affect our daily lives today. Her position and status in Roosevelt’s cabinet has paved the way for more women to enter politics and other fields of study previously believed only men could undertake. The reforms she lobbied for, such as a maximum number of hours one can work without overtime pay, a shortened number of hours one can work, and improved working conditions greatly enhanced worker’s livelihoods. The Fair Labor Standards Act established a minimum wage that still exists today, so even those who work menial jobs still make enough to earn a living. It also set a maximum work week of forty hours, which is still in use today and there is still a minimum working age today. The Social Security Act enables those who are retired and unable to work to earn enough money to continue living relatively comfortably, and still supports them. Many of the reforms that Perkins advocated for are still relevant to work today and as the first female cabinet member, she set a precedent for the role of women in politics.
